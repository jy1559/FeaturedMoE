\begin{abstract}
Sequential recommendation exhibits heterogeneous behavioral regimes, yet most models still process all prefixes through largely shared computation. In practice, some prefixes are governed by stable preference, others by evolving session intent, and others by immediate local transitions. This creates a mismatch between behavioral diversity and shared computation. Mixture-of-experts (MoE) offers a natural mechanism for conditional computation, but hidden-state-driven routing remains limited for this setting: it is often semantically opaque and does not explicitly align expert allocation with different temporal scopes.

We propose \textbf{RouteRec}, a behavior-guided MoE framework for sequential recommendation built around three principles. First, RouteRec derives \emph{portable behavioral cues} from ordinary interaction logs, avoiding dependence on heavy side-information encoders. Second, it applies \emph{coarse-to-fine multi-stage routing} across macro, mid, and micro scopes so that long-range tendency, within-session evolution, and immediate transition cues can influence routing at different granularities. Third, it uses \emph{behavior-guided expert allocation} that decouples routing signals from hidden representations, making expert selection more inspectable and behavior-grounded.

This design treats behavior features as routing controls rather than as yet another representation-fusion channel. As a result, RouteRec preserves strong sequence modeling in the representation path while introducing a lightweight and transferable control interface for conditional computation. Experiments show that RouteRec improves ranking quality on several datasets while remaining competitive on others, and yields more behavior-consistent routing patterns in diagnostic analyses.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Sequential recommendation is usually formulated as next-item prediction over an ordered interaction history, but the underlying behavior is rarely homogeneous. Even for the same user, one prefix may reflect stable preference, another may reflect an evolving within-session goal, and another may be dominated by immediate local exploration. Prior work on sequential and session-based recommendation has repeatedly shown that user behavior mixes multiple regimes rather than following a single stationary pattern \cite{wang2019seqsurvey,wang2021sessionsurvey}.

Strong sequential recommenders model these histories increasingly well \cite{hidasi2016gru4rec,kang2018sasrec,li2020tisasrec,qiu2022duorec,shin2024bsarec,liu2025sigma}. However, most of them still rely on largely shared computation across prefixes that may arise from very different behavioral contexts. This shared-path assumption is practical and often effective on average, but it also creates a modeling mismatch: heterogeneous behavior is acknowledged in the data, while computation remains mostly uniform.

Figure~\ref{fig:intro-behavioral-features} illustrates this gap. Distinct users and sessions can differ in pace, repetition, focus, and local transition patterns, yet standard sequential models expose limited control over how such differences should change the computation path. Existing advances therefore improve representation quality, but they leave the allocation of computation mostly implicit.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/figure1_different_user.pdf}
  \caption{Different users and sessions can require different computational paths.}
  \Description{A problem-motivation figure contrasting different user and session behavior patterns, highlighting that behaviorally different cases are often processed with the same computation path in standard sequential recommendation.}
  \label{fig:intro-behavioral-features}
\end{figure}

Feature-aware and heterogeneity-aware sequential models partially address this issue, but mostly by enriching representations, reweighting attention, or decomposing interests inside a shared computation pipeline. In other words, prior work is not unaware of behavioral heterogeneity; it usually chooses to absorb that heterogeneity into better sequence representations. In contrast, the question we ask here is more direct: can observable behavior cues control \emph{which computation path} should be used? Mixture-of-experts (MoE) is a natural candidate for this purpose \cite{jacobs1991adaptive,jordan1994hme,shazeer2017moe,fedus2022switch}, because it can replace one shared transformation with condition-dependent expert allocation. Yet simply inserting experts is not enough. For sequential recommendation, routing must still answer why specialization is needed in the first place, where routing should differ across temporal evidence, and what signals should drive routing in an interpretable way.

\textbf{Challenge 1: behavioral diversity versus shared computation.} Real prefixes arise from different behavioral regimes, but most sequential recommenders still process them with largely shared computation. This leaves the model with limited ability to specialize computation for persistent preference, evolving session intent, or immediate local transitions. At this point, the first question is simply why routing should exist at all in sequential recommendation. Our answer is that if routing is to be justified beyond generic extra capacity, it should be controlled by portable cues that can be derived from ordinary logs rather than from heavy dataset-specific side branches.

\textbf{Challenge 2: temporal-scope mismatch.} Once routing is introduced, another issue appears immediately: behavioral evidence arrives at different horizons. Long-range history reflects relatively stable tendency, within-session context reflects evolving intent, and short-range context reflects immediate transition pressure. A single routing view is therefore too coarse: it can blur signals that should influence expert allocation at different granularities. The second question is therefore where routing should differ. Our answer is that routing should be aligned explicitly with macro, mid, and micro temporal scopes rather than imposed as one uniform control signal.

\textbf{Challenge 3: opaque hidden-state routing.} Even if multi-stage routing is available, routing is often driven primarily by hidden representations. This is effective, but it makes expert allocation difficult to inspect because the same latent space must both encode sequence content and decide computation paths. The third question is what should drive routing. Our answer is that if routing is meant to reflect behavior, it should be grounded in signals that can be traced back to observable interaction patterns rather than hidden-state geometry alone.

Taken together, these challenges raise three concrete design questions: why routing should exist at all, where it should differ across temporal evidence, and what should drive it in an interpretable way.

We address these challenges with \textbf{RouteRec}, a behavior-guided MoE framework for sequential recommendation. RouteRec is organized around three design axes that map directly to the challenges above. \emph{Portable Behavioral Cues} summarize ordinary logs into compact routing signals, providing a lightweight interface for behavior-aware control. Such cues include, for example, inter-event tempo, category concentration and switching, recurrence patterns, and popularity exposure. \emph{Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing} applies those cues across macro, mid, and micro scopes so that expert allocation follows temporal evidence granularity rather than a single global bias. \emph{Behavior-Guided Expert Allocation} decouples routing signals from hidden representations, so experts transform sequence states while routing remains tied to inspectable behavior cues.

This framing deliberately treats behavioral information as a control interface for conditional computation rather than as another feature-fusion pathway. As a result, RouteRec preserves a strong sequence backbone while exposing a lightweight mechanism for behavior-grounded specialization. The rest of the paper follows this structure: Related Work clarifies where prior studies stop short of these goals, Method formalizes the three design axes, and Experiments test whether compact cues, multi-stage routing, and decoupled expert allocation each matter in practice.

The main contributions of this work are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We identify three front-end challenges for conditional computation in sequential recommendation: the mismatch between behavioral diversity and shared computation, temporal-scope mismatch in routing, and the opacity of hidden-state-only expert allocation.
  \item We propose RouteRec, which combines portable behavioral cues, coarse-to-fine multi-stage routing, and behavior-guided expert allocation to align expert usage with observable behavior.
  \item We design RouteRec to operate from ordinary interaction logs, making the routing interface lightweight, transferable, and suitable for diagnostic analysis across datasets.
  \item We show that RouteRec improves recommendation quality over strong baselines on several datasets and yields more behavior-consistent routing patterns in ablation and diagnostic studies.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Shared-Path Sequential Modeling and Conditional Computation}
Sequential recommendation has progressed from recurrent and convolutional models to graph and Transformer architectures \cite{hidasi2016gru4rec,tang2018caser,wu2019srgnn,kang2018sasrec,sun2019bert4rec}. Recent methods further improve performance with time-aware attention, contrastive learning, and structural inductive bias \cite{li2020tisasrec,xie2020cl4srec,wang2023multiplecontrast,qiu2022duorec,du2023fearec,shin2024bsarec,liu2025sigma}. These methods substantially strengthen sequence representation quality, but they generally preserve one shared computation pathway for behaviorally different prefixes.

Conditional computation and MoE provide a natural response to this limitation. Classical MoE and later sparse routing work show that different inputs need not share the same transformation path \cite{jacobs1991adaptive,jordan1994hme,shazeer2017moe,fedus2022switch}. Recommendation systems have also adopted expert decomposition to improve specialization and capacity \cite{ma2018mmoe,tang2020ple,liu2025fame,bian2023m3srec,zhang2025hm4sr}. However, existing sequential recommendation literature still gives limited attention to the specific question we focus on here: how to let observable behavioral diversity determine computation allocation. RouteRec addresses this gap by building a portable behavior cue interface for routing rather than keeping all heterogeneity inside a shared-path encoder.

\subsection{Multi-Scope Behavioral Signals in Sequential Recommendation}
Many studies explicitly acknowledge that user state is heterogeneous or non-stationary, including short-term intent modeling, multi-interest extraction, and long-short preference decomposition \cite{li2017narm,liu2018stamp,ying2018shan,ma2019hgn,li2019mind,cen2020comirec,quadrana2017hrnn}. Feature-aware sequential recommenders also incorporate timestamps, side information, and auxiliary feature channels to enrich sequence encoding \cite{li2020tisasrec,zhang2019fdsa,xie2022difsr,du2023fearec,bauer2024hybridsession}. Together, these lines of work show that sequential behavior is expressed across multiple temporal scopes and can benefit from richer behavioral evidence.

At the same time, most prior approaches use these signals to refine hidden representations, reweight attention, or fuse side information. That is, they acknowledge multi-scope behavior, but they do not usually turn scope differences into an explicit routing design problem. RouteRec builds on this literature by asking not only how to represent multiple scopes, but also how to assign computation differently across them. Its coarse-to-fine multi-stage routing therefore treats macro, mid, and micro evidence as distinct control views rather than as one pooled feature stream.

\subsection{Expert Routing Semantics and Inspectability}
MoE-based recommendation models show that expert specialization can improve flexibility, but routing semantics often remain unclear. In many architectures, routing is driven by hidden representations, task identities, or learned latent facets \cite{ma2018mmoe,tang2020ple,liu2025fame,bian2023m3srec,zhang2025hm4sr,zhou2022expertchoice,zoph2022stmoe}. This can be effective, yet it leaves a limited explanation of why a particular expert was chosen and whether that choice corresponds to an observable behavioral regime.

For our setting, this interpretability issue matters because RouteRec is not only trying to add capacity; it is trying to specialize computation according to behavior. Prior expert models mainly specialize through latent, task-level, or facet-level signals, whereas RouteRec focuses on observable cues tied to temporal behavioral regimes. This difference matters for the later experiments because it changes not only how routing is computed, but also what kinds of diagnostic questions the model can answer. We therefore separate the role of representations and routing: hidden states remain the content channel transformed by experts, while routing is controlled by portable behavioral cues derived from standard logs.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{RouteRec Overview}
\label{sec:problem-framework}

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes RouteRec. For each user, we observe interactions ordered by time and train the model for next-item prediction. In one training instance, the observed prefix is denoted by $x_{1:t}$ and the target by $x_{t+1}$. Each interaction contains at least an item identity and timestamp, and may additionally include coarse category information depending on the dataset. RouteRec retains a strong sequential backbone for encoding the prefix, but replaces shared feed-forward computation with stage-wise MoE routing.

We first describe RouteRec as a design framework and refer to the fixed paper instantiation only when concrete implementation choices matter. The model uses two signal channels. The representation channel maps the observed prefix to hidden states, which are the content representations transformed by experts. The behavior channel summarizes the same prefix into compact cues derived from ordinary logs. These cues are deliberately lightweight: they are designed as a control interface for routing rather than as another deep side-information branch. In the main text we discuss the selected paper instantiation only when that choice matters for the empirical track, while broader variants and implementation alternatives are deferred to the appendix ablations. Appendix~\ref{app:method-details} collects the corresponding specification details for the selected paper track.

This design directly follows the challenge structure introduced earlier. Challenge~1 motivates portable behavior cues derived from widely available logs. Challenge~2 motivates stage-wise routing over macro, mid, and micro temporal scopes. Challenge~3 motivates decoupling behavior-grounded routing from the expert transformation pathway so that expert allocation can be analyzed in terms of observable behavior. The remainder of this section formalizes how these three ideas are instantiated in one RouteRec architecture.

Let $\mathcal{T}=\{\mathrm{macro},\mathrm{mid},\mathrm{micro}\}$ be the stage set. Let $\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{in},(s)}_t$ denote the hidden state entering stage $s$. At stage $s \in \mathcal{T}$, routing uses a stage-specific behavior feature $\mathbf{f}^{(s)}_t$, while experts consume the current stage input state:
\begin{align}
\boldsymbol{\pi}^{(s)}_t
&=
\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\mathrm{Router}_s(\mathbf{f}^{(s)}_t)/\tau_s\right), \\
\tilde{\mathbf{h}}^{(s)}_t
&=
\sum_{e=1}^{E}\pi^{(s)}_{t,e}\,\mathrm{Expert}^{(s)}_e(\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{in},(s)}_t).
\label{eq:stage-routing}
\end{align}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:stage-routing} is the common stage interface. In the serial RouteRec architecture, each stage consumes the hidden state produced by the previous attention or routing block. Thus, the framework keeps one shared MoE interface, while the paper instantiation specifies how these stage-local states are connected in sequence.

\subsection{Portable Behavioral Cues}
\label{sec:feature-construction}

RouteRec is designed for realistic data availability. It uses signals that are commonly present in interaction logs: item identity, timestamp, optional coarse item-group labels, and corpus-level popularity statistics derived from the training data. We intentionally avoid heavy side-information encoders in the routing branch. Instead, raw signals are converted into compact scalar summaries that are easy to compute, stable across datasets, and interpretable at analysis time.

For session $s_m$ and target position $t$, the stage windows are
\begin{align}
\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{macro}}_t &= [s_{\max(1,m-H)},\dots,s_{m-1}], \\
\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{mid}}_t &= [x_1,\dots,x_{t-1}], \\
\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{micro}}_t &= [x_{\max(1,t-w)},\dots,x_{t-1}],
\end{align}
where $H=5$ and $w=5$ in our main setting. These are the default paper windows used throughout the main experiments, and their role is revisited in the structural ablations in Appendix~\ref{app:stage-layout}. A shared summary operator $\mathcal{A}(\cdot)$ maps each window to a low-dimensional feature vector:
\[
\mathbf{f}^{(s)}_t=\mathcal{A}(\mathcal{R}^{(s)}_t),\qquad s\in\mathcal{T}.
\]

The summary dimensions are grouped into four semantic families: \textit{Tempo}, \textit{Focus}, \textit{Memory}, and \textit{Exposure}. Tempo captures coverage, pace, and interval dynamics. Focus captures concentration and switching behavior over the coarsest item-group proxy available in the dataset, such as product category, venue category, artist grouping, or genre. Memory captures repetition, overlap, and recurrence structure. Exposure captures the level, spread, and drift of interacted popularity. In the fixed paper instantiation, this family template yields a compact 16-dimensional cue bank per stage. When such grouping fields are unavailable, the corresponding focus cues are handled by the fixed missing-signal rule documented in Appendix~\ref{app:method-details}. Representative examples are listed in Table~\ref{tab:appendix-feature-families}.

Importantly, the feature template is shared across stages, but each stage reads it through a different temporal lens. Macro features reflect persistent tendency and broad preference shape. Mid features track evolving intent inside the current session. Micro features emphasize immediate cues around the prediction target. This shared-template, stage-specific-view design keeps the feature branch compact while preserving temporal specificity.

This feature design is intentionally more structured than generic feature fusion. The goal is not to expose as many handcrafted statistics as possible, but to construct a small cue bank whose dimensions remain semantically legible and transportable across datasets. We therefore use stage-specific summaries with a consistent family template, rather than dataset-specific deep side branches. This family structure is treated as a semantic prior rather than as an immutable ontology, and Section~\ref{sec:stage-structure} tests whether that organization matters beyond using an unstructured bag of scalars. The appendix records representative cue definitions and the fixed paper setting, while more exhaustive column-level formulas can remain outside the main text because they are engineering details rather than the conceptual core of RouteRec.

\subsection{Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing}

Although the router formula is shared, stages are not redundant. They differ in temporal evidence, routing granularity, and placement inside the backbone. In the fixed paper instantiation, the serial layout is
\[
\text{self-attention} \rightarrow \text{macro routing block} \rightarrow \text{mid routing block} \rightarrow \text{self-attention} \rightarrow \text{micro routing block}.
\]
Let $\mathbf{h}^{(0)}_t$ denote the hidden state after the first attention block, $\mathbf{h}^{(1)}_t$ after macro routing, $\mathbf{h}^{(2)}_t$ after mid routing, and $\mathbf{h}^{(3)}_t$ after the second attention block. Macro, mid, and micro therefore consume $\mathbf{h}^{(0)}_t$, $\mathbf{h}^{(1)}_t$, and $\mathbf{h}^{(3)}_t$, respectively. The first attention layer builds a contextualized prefix representation before any routing decision is made. Macro and mid then operate consecutively because both provide slower sequence-level control over closely related contextual states: macro reflects broader recent-history tendency, whereas mid refines the evolving intent of the current session. In our architecture sweep, inserting another attention layer between them increased depth without yielding a comparably distinct temporal view; the issue is not depth for its own sake, but whether the extra update creates a meaningfully different temporal control view. By contrast, the second attention layer is placed immediately before micro so that the final local router sees a refreshed token interaction state before prediction. Appendix~\ref{app:stage-layout} reports the corresponding stage-order and layout ablations for this selected architectural choice.

\paragraph{Macro and mid (sequence-wise routing).}
Macro and mid compute one routing distribution per sequence from their respective windows, then broadcast it over valid positions inside the sequence. Macro uses broader cross-session history; mid uses within-session prefix evidence. This setting emphasizes stable allocation for slower-changing context, where token-wise routing tended to add variance without introducing an equally meaningful control resolution in our ablations.

\paragraph{Micro (position-wise routing).}
Micro computes routing per token near the prediction target using short-horizon context. This enables fast adaptation to immediate transitions without changing the router form itself.

Formally, let $\bar{\mathbf{f}}_i^{\,(s)}$ denote the sequence-level cue summary used for sequence-wise routing at stage $s$. Then macro and mid use
\[
\begin{aligned}
\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}_i^{\,(s)}
&=
\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\mathrm{Router}_s(\bar{\mathbf{f}}_i^{\,(s)})/\tau_s\right),
\qquad s\in\{\mathrm{macro},\mathrm{mid}\}, \\
\boldsymbol{\pi}^{(s)}_{i,t}
&=
\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}_i^{\,(s)}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Micro instead applies the same router form directly to position-specific cues,
\[
\boldsymbol{\pi}^{(\mathrm{micro})}_{i,t}
=
\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\mathrm{Router}_{\mathrm{micro}}(\mathbf{f}^{(\mathrm{micro})}_{i,t})/\tau_{\mathrm{micro}}\right).
\]
Hence, RouteRec's coarse-to-fine behavior comes from stage evidence scope and update frequency, while the underlying router composition remains constant. The stages are therefore not repeated copies of the same router; they correspond to different temporal horizons at which behavioral evidence becomes informative for computation allocation.

\subsection{Behavior-Guided Expert Allocation}

The final RouteRec architecture uses one shared scalar-group conditional composition at every stage. Experts are partitioned into $G$ groups with $C$ experts per group, so $E=GC$. For stage $s$ and position $t$, the router produces
\begin{align}
p^{(s)}_{t,g} &= \mathrm{softmax}_g\!\left(z^{(s)}_{t,g}\right), \\
r^{(s)}_{t,g,c} &= \mathrm{softmax}_c\!\left(u^{(s)}_{t,g,c}\right),
\end{align}
where $p^{(s)}_{t,g}$ is a group-level routing distribution and $r^{(s)}_{t,g,c}$ is a conditional within-group routing distribution. The final expert probability is their product:
\begin{equation}
\pi^{(s)}_{t,g,c}=p^{(s)}_{t,g}\,r^{(s)}_{t,g,c},
\label{eq:group-conditional-product}
\end{equation}
followed by flattening $(g,c)$ to expert index $e$. Eq.~\eqref{eq:group-conditional-product} is the same for macro, mid, and micro.

In the paper instantiation, the group structure is organized around the four cue families, while capacity is expanded by multiple experts within each group. This alignment operates only at the router-prior level: it defines a semantic prior over expert organization rather than a hard hand assignment of individual cues or cue dimensions to individual experts. The experts themselves remain fully learned.

In the main configuration, all three stages use the same feature-driven group-conditional router. A group-level scalar router first determines which semantic control view is most relevant. A conditional intra-group router then distributes probability mass among the experts inside that group. In plain terms, RouteRec first selects the family-level control view and then allocates capacity within that view. This is the core reason we describe the method as behavior-guided allocation: the router is controlled by the cue bank, while hidden states remain the objects transformed by the experts. We choose dense soft mixing in this paper because it makes stage-wise specialization easier to compare and diagnose across datasets \cite{zoph2022stmoe,zhou2022expertchoice}; sparse top-$k$ routing remains an orthogonal systems choice outside the present scope.

This unified composition is important for two reasons. First, it gives all stages a shared structural prior, which reduces stage-specific router engineering and simplifies debugging. Second, it keeps diagnostics comparable across stages because differences in routing behavior can be attributed to evidence scope rather than formula changes.

In RouteRec, routing is driven by behavior cues, while hidden states remain dedicated to expert transformation in Eq.~\eqref{eq:stage-routing}. This preserves the routing-representation decoupling principle that underlies our approach.

\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{sec:training-objective}

RouteRec is trained with a standard next-item objective and two router-side regularizers in the main paper configuration: route consistency and z-loss. The first regularizer shapes routing semantics, and the second stabilizes routing numerics.

Let $\mathbf{z}$ be final logits and $y$ be the target item. The main loss is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}
=
-\log \frac{\exp z_y}{\sum_{j}\exp z_j}.
\]
For route consistency, we first compute a sample-level routing distribution at each stage by averaging token-level routing weights:
\[
\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}_i^{\,s}
=
\frac{1}{L_i}\sum_{t=1}^{L_i}\boldsymbol{\pi}_{i,t}^{\,s}
\]
where $L_i$ is the valid sequence length. We also form a stage-level cue summary
\[
\bar{\mathbf{f}}_i^{\,s}
=
\frac{1}{L_i}\sum_{t=1}^{L_i}\mathbf{f}_{i,t}^{\,s}.
\]
For each stage $s$, we construct a pair set $\mathcal{P}_s$ from the $k_{\mathrm{nn}}$ nearest non-self samples in the mini-batch according to cosine similarity between these stage-level cue summaries, and minimize
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}
\sum_{s \in \mathcal{T}}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_s|}
\sum_{(i,j)\in \mathcal{P}_s}
\mathrm{JSD}\!\left(\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}_i^{\,s}, \bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}_j^{\,s}\right),
\]
which encourages neighboring behavioral states to produce compatible routing distributions. In other words, semantically similar inputs should not induce arbitrarily different routing behavior. Appendix~\ref{app:method-details} reports the neighbor setting used in the paper instantiation. For macro and mid, the sample-level average reduces to the broadcast sequence-wise route; we keep the averaging notation for a unified treatment across stages.

To stabilize gating numerics, we adopt z-loss on the pre-softmax expert logits $a_{i,t,e}^{\,s}$~\cite{zoph2022stmoe}:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{z}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}
\sum_{s \in \mathcal{T}}
\frac{1}{N_s}
\sum_{(i,t)\in\mathcal{V}_s}
\left(
\log \sum_{e=1}^{E}\exp a_{i,t,e}^{\,s}
\right)^2,
\]
where $\mathcal{V}_s$ is the set of valid positions and $N_s=|\mathcal{V}_s|$. This term discourages excessive logit scale and helps prevent unstable, overly sharp routing. The final objective is
\[
\mathcal{L}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{cons}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
+
\lambda_{z}\,\mathcal{L}_{z},
\]
where $\lambda_{\mathrm{cons}}$ and $\lambda_z$ are trade-off coefficients. This objective jointly optimizes prediction quality, semantic consistency of routing, and numerical stability of expert selection.
Unlike classical MoE setups that explicitly enforce uniform expert traffic, the selected main configuration does not include a load-balancing term in the final paper runs. Our goal here is not uniform usage for its own sake, but stable and behavior-aligned specialization. Appendix~\ref{app:objective} reports objective variants including optional balance regularization.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}


\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We evaluate RouteRec on six public datasets. These datasets cover e-commerce, location recommendation, short-video consumption, music listening, movie preference, and anonymous clickstream behavior. The six corpora are Beauty, Foursquare, KuaiRec, LastFM, ML-1M, and Retail Rocket. Appendix~B summarizes their provenance and processed statistics.

Across datasets, we follow a sessionized temporal evaluation protocol assembled from established practices in session-based and session-aware recommendation rather than claiming a single canonical raw-sequence benchmark~\cite{jannach2022sessions,quadrana2017hrnn,latifi2021sessionaware}. Interactions are first ordered chronologically, converted to sessions with inactivity-based heuristics, and then filtered to remove sessions shorter than $5$ and items with fewer than $3$ interactions, which is consistent with common session-based benchmark stabilization pipelines~\cite{li2017narm,wu2019srgnn}. We use a 30-minute threshold for Foursquare, KuaiRec, LastFM, ML-1M, and Retail Rocket, which matches common practice in dense timestamped logs and later session-aware studies~\cite{quadrana2017hrnn,lacic2020jobsessions}, whereas Beauty uses a 14-day threshold because review timestamps are much sparser than click, play, or check-in logs. This follows the broader observation that sparse or item-poor domains may require task-dependent session definitions rather than a universal short timeout~\cite{borgbruun2022insurance}. Beauty is constructed from Amazon Beauty reviews and additionally excludes interactions with rating below $3$.

For transparency, the reported KuaiRec and LastFM results are obtained from processed sampled subsets rather than the full raw corpora: specifically, we use a 20\% KuaiRec subset and a 3\% LastFM subset, and we mark these datasets explicitly in the result tables. Sessions are then sorted by start time and split chronologically into 70\%/15\%/15\% train/validation/test partitions. This is a deliberate methodological choice: sessions are the unit of evaluation because the paper studies session-aware behavioral routing, the split preserves temporal order at the session level, and the held-out construction is designed to keep the benchmark auditable rather than to mimic a raw-sequence leaderboard directly. Because recent work has shown that split construction can materially affect offline conclusions and may induce leakage when chronology is ignored, we keep the temporal protocol explicit and reserve the full preprocessing details for Appendix~B~\cite{ji2023leakage,gusak2025timesplit}.

Our main comparison uses the seen-target evaluation view. This choice is deliberate: ID-based next-item recommenders cannot rank items absent from the training vocabulary, and session-aware benchmarks commonly treat unseen targets as a supplementary rather than primary evaluation view~\cite{latifi2021sessionaware,li2017narm,wu2019srgnn}. Concretely, held-out cleanup removes unseen non-target rows from the primary files, while unseen targets are retained only for supplementary accounting rather than the main comparison. Appendix~B gives the exact held-out cleanup procedure, the processed dataset statistics, the tuning protocol, and the full $k\in\{5,10,20\}$ tables.

The baselines include GRU4Rec, SASRec, TiSASRec, DuoRec, FEARec, BSARec, FAME, DIF-SR, and FDSA~\cite{hidasi2016gru4rec,kang2018sasrec,li2020tisasrec,qiu2022duorec,du2023fearec,shin2024bsarec,liu2025fame,xie2022difsr,zhang2019fdsa}. All methods are trained within the same optimization family, with matched early-stopping rules and comparable capacity ranges. In the comparison tables, each baseline is represented by the run attaining the highest mean validation score over the nine reported metrics, and we report the corresponding test metrics. RouteRec is reported from the best validation-selected configuration considered in this study under the same criterion.

Given that ranking conclusions in sessionized sequential recommendation can change with both protocol construction and optimization quality~\cite{latifi2021sessionaware,latifi2022seqstudy,ji2023leakage,gusak2025timesplit}, we treat tuning as part of the experimental protocol rather than as a secondary implementation detail. Concretely, each model is tuned within a predefined model-specific discrete grid covering standard optimization hyperparameters and a small number of architecture-specific controls. The admissible values are fixed in advance; when adaptive search is used for efficiency, it is applied only over these predeclared candidate sets, following the same validation files, early-stopping rule, and selection criterion for all methods~\cite{galuzzi2020bayesopt}. The intended comparison is therefore transparent and controlled: adaptive search is used only as an efficient implementation over fixed candidate sets, not as open-ended retuning. Appendix~B summarizes the bounded candidate grids and trial budgets used to audit this procedure.

All methods are trained and evaluated on the same processed train/validation/test files. RouteRec does not access different held-out sessions or external metadata at evaluation time; its routing cues are computed from the same observed interaction histories that define the benchmark instances. The intended comparison is therefore a sessionized, behavior-aware next-item setting under a shared temporal protocol, not a claim about direct comparability to canonical raw-sequence leaderboards.

The main experimental section is organized around five questions: \textbf{Q1)} whether RouteRec improves ranking quality under the shared protocol, \textbf{Q2)} what should control routing, \textbf{Q3)} why the three-part design is effective, \textbf{Q4)} whether lightweight cues are sufficient in practice, and \textbf{Q5)} whether the learned routing patterns align with behavioral regimes. This organization keeps the main text focused on the core empirical claims, while the appendix provides broader controls, definitions, and supplementary analyses.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
RouteRec starts from a simple hypothesis: when behavioral evidence changes, the computation path should change as well. The experiments support this view from three main angles. First, RouteRec improves next-item ranking most clearly on behaviorally dynamic datasets and remains competitive on several others, although the advantage narrows in more preference-stable settings such as ML-1M. Second, the gain is tied to the routing signal and the staged design rather than to indiscriminate expert insertion. Third, lightweight cues derived from ordinary interaction logs can preserve a meaningful share of the benefit, which keeps the method practical even when side information is limited.

Taken together, these results suggest that compact behavioral cues provide a useful control interface for conditional computation in recommendation, rather than merely another feature-fusion pathway. The main contribution is therefore the combination of three design axes, portable behavioral cues, coarse-to-fine multi-stage routing, and behavior-guided expert allocation, into one coherent behavior-conditioned computation design. Future work can build on this interface with broader transfer regimes, richer but still robust cue construction, stronger sparse-routing variants, and tighter deployment-aware constraints such as latency, continual adaptation, and router calibration.

For reproducibility, the protocol is summarized as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Sort all interactions chronologically within each dataset.
  \item Reconstruct sessions with a dataset-specific inactivity threshold.
  \item Iteratively prune sessions with length below $5$ and items with support below $3$ until the constraints are simultaneously satisfied.
  \item Sort the resulting sessions by session start time.
  \item Assign the earliest 70\% of sessions to train and treat the latest 30\% as a held-out tail.
  \item Build validation and test from the held-out tail under chronology-preserving cleanup.
  \item Report seen-target metrics as the primary view and keep unseen-target behavior as supplementary accounting.
\end{enumerate}

Beauty and Retail Rocket stress sparse e-commerce revisit behavior, Foursquare and KuaiRec emphasize stronger short-horizon intent changes, and LastFM and ML-1M provide longer preference-driven histories. This spread makes it possible to examine whether behavior-guided routing is primarily beneficial in highly dynamic settings or remains competitive when preference structure is more stable.

Signal availability also differs in a way that matters for routing. Beauty exposes item, time, and product-category cues after low-rating removal. Foursquare includes item, timestamp, and venue-category structure with bursty check-in dynamics. KuaiRec offers item, time, and coarse content-side metadata in short-video sessions with strong short-term intent shifts. LastFM provides repeated listening histories with artist-side signals, while ML-1M contributes longer preference-driven sequences with genre information. Retail Rocket is the sparsest side-information setting, relying mainly on anonymous event streams with item and time signals.

We position our setup as a sessionized sequential evaluation protocol assembled from established components, not as the canonical raw-sequence protocol for sequential recommendation~\cite{jannach2022sessions,quadrana2017hrnn,latifi2021sessionaware}. Sessions are reconstructed post hoc with inactivity heuristics, which is standard practice when logs do not provide explicit session boundaries~\cite{jannach2022sessions}. The thresholds are adapted to the logging regime of each corpus: Foursquare, KuaiRec, LastFM, ML-1M, and Retail Rocket use a 30-minute inactivity threshold, consistent with dense-log session-aware practice~\cite{quadrana2017hrnn,lacic2020jobsessions}, whereas Beauty uses a 14-day threshold because review timestamps are substantially more diffuse. More generally, this threshold choice reflects the observation that sparse and item-poor domains often require longer or more domain-specific notions of a session than dense clickstream data~\cite{borgbruun2022insurance}. After sessionization, we repeatedly remove sessions shorter than $5$ and items with fewer than $3$ interactions until no further pruning is required. This iterative filtering follows the same general rationale as common session-based benchmark preprocessing, where short sessions and very low-support items are filtered to stabilize next-item supervision and evaluation~\cite{li2017narm,wu2019srgnn}.

Session splits are constructed at the session level after sorting by session start time. The earliest 70\% of sessions are assigned to training, and the remaining 30\% are treated as a held-out tail. This tail is first divided into validation and test using stratification over three factors: whether the target item already appears in the prefix, the target item's popularity bucket with respect to the training split, and the session-length bucket. For the final evaluation files, we then merge the held-out sessions, restore chronological order, and re-split them contiguously into equal validation and test halves. This chronology-preserving design is meant to reduce leakage and keep the offline protocol aligned with temporal deployment order~\cite{ji2023leakage,gusak2025timesplit}.

For the primary evaluation view, we report seen-target metrics only. Concretely, in the held-out files, non-target rows whose items are unseen in training are removed, and unseen targets are retained only for supplementary accounting rather than the main comparison. This matches long-standing session-based and session-aware evaluation practice for ID-based recommenders, which cannot rank targets outside the training vocabulary and therefore typically separate unseen-target behavior from the primary benchmark table~\cite{latifi2021sessionaware,li2017narm,wu2019srgnn}. We report HR, NDCG, and MRR at $k\in\{5,10,20\}$ for all methods.

For the result tables, each baseline is represented by the run with the highest mean validation score over the nine reported metrics, and RouteRec is reported from the best validation-selected configuration under the same selection rule.

All methods use the same processed train/validation/test partitions. RouteRec's additional macro, mid, and micro cues are deterministic summaries derived from those same observed histories, so the comparison should be read as evaluating different ways of exploiting the shared sessionized interaction context rather than different access to held-out information.

\subsection{Model Selection and Tuning Protocol}

Because this benchmark is built from a sessionized temporal protocol rather than a canonical raw-sequence leaderboard, the model-selection procedure is stated explicitly. Prior benchmark studies have shown that both baseline quality and model ranking can change materially once methods are re-evaluated under a common framework and retuned under a shared protocol~\cite{latifi2021sessionaware,latifi2022seqstudy}. Accordingly, the comparison does not rely on paper defaults alone.

Our reporting protocol follows three simple rules. First, every model is tuned within a predefined bounded search space that is fixed before the final comparison: learning rate is sampled log-uniformly within a declared dataset-specific interval, while the remaining hyperparameters are drawn from small predeclared candidate sets. Second, these bounded spaces cover a shared core of optimization hyperparameters together with a small number of model-specific controls, so that the search is broad enough to avoid trivial under-tuning but still budgeted tightly enough to remain comparable across methods. Third, the final reported result for every model is the test performance of the configuration that maximizes the mean validation score over the nine reported metrics. When adaptive search is used for computational efficiency, it is restricted to the same predeclared bounds and candidate sets and should therefore be read as an efficient implementation of a controlled bounded search rather than as open-ended retuning~\cite{galuzzi2020bayesopt}.

The final paper track therefore uses bounded search spaces rather than unconstrained retuning. In the paper-facing comparison, baselines and RouteRec are aligned to the same shared optimization pool, and model families differ only through a small number of architecture-specific additions. For RouteRec, the selected three-stage routing layout remains fixed, but depth, hidden dropout, and routing-side controls are still tuned within bounded candidate sets.
