RouteRec revised draft review notes (reviewer-style, critical)

Purpose
- Evaluate the revised PDF as if I were a first-pass reviewer reading in order.
- Focus on what feels stronger now, what still raises skepticism, and what to fix before submission.
- Include additional reference candidates and links so another model/person can continue digging.

==================================================
0. One-line overall verdict
==================================================

Current impression:
- The paper is noticeably stronger than before in framing and in making the experimental protocol explicit.
- The central idea is now understandable: hidden states are for representation, behavior cues are for routing, and macro/mid/micro provide temporal scopes.
- However, the paper still has several reviewer-facing risks that could seriously weaken acceptance chances unless fixed:
  1) protocol/fairness ambiguity,
  2) overclaiming relative to the actual win profile,
  3) missing or unresolved citations/placeholders,
  4) insufficiently explicit evidence that gains are not an artifact of the sessionized setup,
  5) not enough “hard proof” for the strongest causal claims in the experiment text.

If I were scoring loosely right now:
- novelty/idea clarity: decent to good
- writing/positioning: improved a lot
- experimental credibility: still the main risk
- acceptance confidence: would depend heavily on whether the protocol/fairness issues are cleaned up and whether appendix evidence is actually complete

1. Reviewer-style reading flow: what I think while reading
==================================================

------------------------------
1.1 Abstract / Introduction
------------------------------

What works:
- The opening is clean. “behavioral diversity vs shared computation” is easy to understand.
- The hidden-state-only routing critique is plausible and intuitive.
- The behavior-guided routing pitch is readable and not too bloated.
- Figure 1 helps quickly communicate the specialization motivation.

What I would think as a reviewer:
- “Okay, this is a seqrec MoE paper, but the key twist is not just MoE: it is behavior-guided routing with interpretable cues.”
- “Good, but now I will expect the experiments to really prove three things:
  (a) routing signal matters,
  (b) stage design matters,
  (c) behavior cues are semantically meaningful rather than decorative.”
- “Because the intro emphasizes controllability/diagnosis, I will expect unusually strong diagnostic evidence, not just small metric gains.”

Potential concern already forming:
- The intro makes a strong conceptual case, so if the empirical gains are only mixed or small, I will scrutinize whether the interpretation is stronger than the ranking improvement.

------------------------------
1.2 Related Work
------------------------------

What works:
- The 4-way structure is neat and easy to follow.
- The positioning “heterogeneity as routing problem rather than only representation problem” is clear.

What I would think:
- “This is fine, but still a little too compressed. If the paper leans on session/user heterogeneity, I want at least one or two more explicit bridges to session-intent / multi-interest lines.”
- “There is enough coverage for baseline families, but the story could still be strengthened with one more explicit session-intent baseline or reference.”

What is still missing / thin:
- Session-intent / multi-interest / long-short state modeling is mentioned, but not very concretely connected to why RouteRec is different.
- The MoE subsection is still a bit broad; it would benefit from one sentence separating:
  - recommendation MoE in general,
  - seqrec-specific MoE,
  - behavior-guided routing as your actual niche.

------------------------------
1.3 Preliminary + Method
------------------------------

What works:
- The preliminary section is defensible; it reduces notation shock.
- Figure 2 is much better for comprehension.
- The method structure is readable: overview -> features -> routing -> stage instantiation -> objective.
- The routing/representation separation is clear.
- The four cue families are named in a way that is intuitive enough for readers.

What I would think:
- “I understand the architecture. Good.”
- “But I now want harder specifics: what exactly is in A(.), how many dimensions, what is shared vs stage-specific, and how sensitive is performance to those choices?”
- “The stages are understandable semantically, but the exact reason macro and mid share the same router form while only differing in granularity may still feel a bit under-argued.”

Main reviewer concern here:
- The method text says the architecture uses one unified scalar-group conditional router across all stages. That is good for elegance, but it also weakens the feeling that stage design itself is deeply principled unless the experiments prove stage-specific necessity very convincingly.
- In other words: the paper currently explains why multiple scopes make sense, but it still needs stronger empirical proof that macro/mid/micro are not just a nice decomposition narrative.

Another concern:
- The behavior features are described at a high level only. That is okay for main text, but then appendix must be concrete enough that a skeptical reviewer feels the cue bank is not hand-wavy.
- Right now, if appendix feature definitions are not explicit enough, a reviewer may think: “This is an interpretable-cues paper that does not fully specify the cues.”

------------------------------
1.4 Experimental setup
------------------------------

This is where reviewer skepticism spikes.

What improved:
- The paper now explicitly says this is a “sessionized temporal evaluation protocol assembled from established practices” rather than pretending it is canonical.
- That is the right tone.
- Explicitly marking KuaiRec and LastFM as sampled subsets is good and honest.
- Explicitly saying “seen-target view” is the main comparison is also better than hiding it.

What I would immediately notice negatively:
- There are still unresolved placeholders like “[? ? ? ]”, “[31? ]”, etc.
- This is a major professionalism issue. It signals the experiment section is still unfinished.
- Appendix also still has missing citations and even “Table ??” in the objective section.
- A reviewer will absolutely notice this and may infer that the protocol justification is not fully nailed down.

What I would think in substance:
- “Fine, they are not claiming canonical seqrec protocol. Good.”
- “But if this is a custom sessionized protocol, fairness becomes the first question.”
- “Do all baselines receive comparable information?”
- “Does RouteRec benefit uniquely from cross-session macro cues while baselines effectively operate on only current-session prefixes?”
- “If yes, then the comparison may not isolate routing quality; it may partly reflect information asymmetry.”

This is probably the single biggest methodological risk.

Why this matters:
- RouteRec explicitly uses macro past-session signals.
- If baselines are fed only current-session examples after sessionization, they may be structurally disadvantaged.
- Even if every model is trained on the same processed files, the information usage is not necessarily comparable.

If I were a reviewer, I would want one of the following:
1) a strong explicit statement that all baselines also receive the same available history in a comparable serialized form, or
2) a second comparison block where strong baselines are history-augmented in some simple but fair way, or
3) a very clear problem-definition argument that the benchmark is intentionally “session-local prediction with optional cross-session side cues”, and thus the baseline family is being evaluated under that exact problem.

Without that, I would keep thinking:
- “Maybe RouteRec wins partly because the benchmark favors its design.”

Other setup concerns:
- Using sampled subsets for KuaiRec and LastFM is honest, but still risky. I would wonder:
  - Why exactly 20% and 3%?
  - Are results stable across different random samples/seeds?
  - Are these subsets stratified or arbitrary?
  - Could RouteRec’s gains be sample-sensitive?
- The main text says full details are in Appendix A, but then Appendix A also still has unresolved placeholders.
- The held-out cleanup / stratification / merge-resplit procedure is nontrivial. A reviewer may think it is too complex unless you explain very clearly why each step exists.

------------------------------
1.5 Main results
------------------------------

What works:
- Table 1 is cleaner now.
- “Seen-target test results under the sessionized temporal protocol” is a much better caption than pretending it is generic seqrec.
- Avg. Rank is a nice compact summary.

What I would think when reading the numbers:
- “The method clearly helps on Beauty and somewhat on KuaiRec, is competitive on Retail Rocket, but is not the best on ML-1M and not clearly dominant on Foursquare/LastFM.”
- “So this is not a universal across-the-board win story.”
- “The paper should therefore avoid sounding like it consistently improves everywhere.”

This means the narrative must be more careful.

Good version of the claim:
- RouteRec helps most in behaviorally dynamic / heterogeneous / concentrated regimes.
- It is competitive elsewhere.

Risky/overstated version of the claim:
- RouteRec broadly improves seqrec across datasets.

A skeptical reviewer will notice:
- ML-1M is a pretty visible negative case.
- LastFM is basically competitive, not clearly better.
- Retail Rocket is tiny gain territory.

So the paper should proactively own this pattern and interpret it:
- Maybe behavior-guided routing is strongest when session-local behavioral regime shifts matter.
- Maybe in very long, preference-stable settings the advantage narrows or even disappears.

If you say this first, reviewers may accept the nonuniform result profile much more easily.

Another issue:
- Avg. Rank is helpful, but some reviewers dislike rank summaries over many correlated metrics.
- Keep it, but do not rely on it too heavily.
- Consider adding a small “wins / second-best / competitive” count or average relative improvement summary.

Strong recommendation:
- Add significance testing or at least repeated-run mean±std for main methods, especially on datasets where margins are tiny.
- Otherwise a reviewer may say the results on Retail Rocket / Foursquare / LastFM are within noise.

------------------------------
1.6 Routing-control, staged-design, and cue ablations
------------------------------

What works:
- These are exactly the right experiments to include.
- The experiment structure matches the paper’s causal story:
  routing signal -> stage design -> cue sufficiency.

What I would think critically:
- “Good experiment categories, but the text is still more interpretive than evidentiary.”
- Example: “Across all three views, the same pattern is expected.” This phrasing is weak. It sounds like the paper is telling me what should happen, not what actually happened.
- The text needs sharper, more concrete takeaways with actual effect sizes or at least specific directional findings.

For example, instead of:
- “the same pattern is expected”
use something like:
- “Removing macro causes the largest average drop on X/Y datasets, while dense replacement fails to match the full staged design, confirming that gains come from scope-specific routing rather than merely more experts.”

Likewise for cue ablation:
- Right now the text says removing category/time lowers performance and sequence-only still retains substantial gain.
- That is good, but a reviewer may want exact retained proportion or average delta.
- Quantify it.

What still hurts a lot:

As a reviewer, unfinished appendix artifacts damage confidence disproportionately. They make me wonder whether some of the promised evidence is still aspirational.

2. Biggest reviewer risks, ranked by severity
==================================================

[Risk 1] Baseline fairness under the sessionized protocol
- This is the most serious issue.
- RouteRec uses macro past-session signals.
- It is not yet fully clear whether baselines get equivalent access to past-session information or whether they are effectively evaluated on weaker information.
- Even if the protocol is shared, the informational comparison may not be apples-to-apples.

What to do:
- In main text or appendix, explicitly state what each baseline sees.
- Ideally add one fairer auxiliary comparison:
  - history-augmented SASRec / GRU4Rec / TiSASRec by concatenating or packing prior sessions into a capped history sequence, OR
  - a “session-only” comparison family vs a “history-available” comparison family.
- If you cannot add new baselines, at minimum clearly argue the problem setting: “current-session prediction under session-aware side cues,” and state the comparison scope honestly.

[Risk 2] Unfinished placeholders / missing citations / incomplete appendix references
- This is an immediate red flag.
- The experiment section still has citation placeholders and the appendix has unresolved placeholders and “Table ??”.
- Reviewers may treat this as sloppiness in the most important section.

What to do:
- Fix all placeholders before anything else.
- This is zero-science-cost, high-review-impact work.

[Risk 3] Overclaiming relative to mixed result profile
- The gains are real in some datasets, but the story is not “wins everywhere.”
- ML-1M is a visible miss.
- LastFM/Foursquare are more like competitive than clearly superior.

What to do:
- Reframe the main result story around regime dependence.
- Explicitly say RouteRec is strongest when behavior regimes are dynamic / concentrated / session-sensitive.
- Treat stable long-history domains as boundary conditions, not embarrassments.

[Risk 4] Sessionized benchmark protocol is still complex and may feel custom-built
- The paper is now honest that the protocol is assembled from prior components, which is good.
- But the more complex the split/cleanup pipeline is, the more a reviewer wants proof it is not over-engineered to help the method.

What to do:
- Add a small appendix pseudo-code or bullet pipeline.
- Add protocol robustness evidence if possible:
  - alternate threshold sanity check,
  - seen-target vs all-target supplementary table,
  - maybe one simpler split variant if feasible.

[Risk 5] The strongest claims need harder quantitative support
- “behavior-consistent expert usage”, “not simple capacity scaling”, “lightweight cues are enough” are plausible but need more explicit numbers.
- Right now some text sounds a little too narrative.

What to do:
- In main text, quantify at least once per major claim.
- Examples:
  - average gain of behavior-only router over hidden-only router,
  - average drop after removing macro,
  - retained fraction under sequence-only cues,
  - entropy/effective expert count summary.

4. What I would rewrite in the experiment narrative
==================================================

------------------------------
4.1 Main setting paragraph: recommended tone
------------------------------

Good tone:
- “We evaluate under a sessionized temporal protocol assembled from established session-based and session-aware benchmarking components, because our goal is to study behavior-guided routing at the session level rather than to claim a new canonical raw-sequence benchmark.”

Why this works:
- honest
- defensible
- narrows the claim to the actual problem setting

------------------------------
4.2 Main results paragraph: recommended tone
------------------------------

Recommended structure:
1) State the overall pattern honestly.
2) Interpret where it helps most.
3) Separate “competitive elsewhere” from “wins strongly here.”

Suggested shape:
- “RouteRec is strongest on Beauty and KuaiRec, remains competitive on Retail Rocket, Foursquare, and LastFM, and is weaker on ML-1M. This pattern suggests that behavior-guided routing is most beneficial when short-horizon regime variation is pronounced, while its advantage narrows in more preference-stable long-history settings.”

This is much stronger than trying to make everything sound positive.

------------------------------
4.3 Results-analysis paragraph that is currently missing
------------------------------

Add a paragraph like this:
- “Because the protocol is sessionized and temporally split, the reported numbers should be interpreted as performance under a behavior-sensitive session-aware benchmark rather than as directly comparable to canonical raw-sequence results reported elsewhere. All methods are compared under the same processed protocol; the contribution of RouteRec is therefore in how it uses the available behavior structure, not in access to a different evaluation target.”

But only say the last sentence if it is actually true for baseline information access.

------------------------------
4.4 Appendix protocol paragraph: make it more algorithmic
------------------------------

Right now Appendix A is better than before, but still a bit prose-heavy.

Better form:
Step 1. Sort interactions by timestamp.
Step 2. Reconstruct sessions with dataset-specific inactivity threshold.
Step 3. Iteratively prune sessions with length < 5 and items with support < 3 until convergence.
Step 4. Sort sessions by session start time.
Step 5. Split earliest 70% to train, treat remaining 30% as held-out tail.
Step 6. Build validation/test from held-out tail under chronology-preserving cleanup.
Step 7. Report seen-target metrics as main view; keep unseen-target statistics separately.

This makes the protocol feel reproducible rather than ad hoc.

10. Bottom line
==================================================

This version is clearly better. The main idea is now understandable, and the experimental protocol is no longer hidden.

But if I were a reviewer, I would still be most worried about whether the sessionized protocol and macro cross-session cues make the comparison slightly favorable to RouteRec unless baseline access/fairness is spelled out very carefully.

So the best next move is not to keep polishing prose in the abstract/introduction. The best next move is to make the experimental section feel airtight:
- finish all citations/placeholders,
- clarify fairness,
- reduce overclaiming,
- and add one or two robustness/fairness checks that make skepticism expensive.
