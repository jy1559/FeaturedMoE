RouteRec integration plan (manuscript + appendix + refs)

0. Quick status
- The uploaded PDF versions (9) and (10) appear identical in content, so there is no real manuscript conflict to reconcile between those two files.
- That means integration should be handled as: keep the current manuscript body as the base, then selectively merge in (i) the newer abstract/introduction/method framing from the other agent and (ii) the stronger appendix/method-validation/tuning references.

1. What the final paper should feel like
The cleanest final story is still the one discussed in the original meeting:

front-end challenges -> three method design axes -> reviewer-facing experiments -> appendix validation

More concretely:
- Challenge 1: behavioral diversity vs shared computation
- Challenge 2: temporal-scope mismatch
- Challenge 3: opaque hidden-state routing

These should map almost one-to-one to:
- Portable Behavioral Cues
- Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing
- Behavior-Guided Expert Allocation

And then the experiments should mirror them:
- Q1: overall competitiveness
- Q2: what should control routing?
- Q3: why is the three-part design meaningful?
- Q4: are simple / widely available cues enough?
- Q5: do routing patterns align with behavioral regimes?

This alignment is already mostly present in the current manuscript. The goal of integration is to make that mapping tighter and more explicit, not to introduce another parallel storyline.

2. What to keep from the current manuscript base
Keep these parts as the main backbone of the paper:

2.1 Abstract / Introduction tone
The current abstract-introduction package is already in a good place because it:
- starts from heterogeneous behavioral regimes,
- raises the routing question directly,
- states the three principles clearly,
- and frames RouteRec as a control interface rather than another feature-fusion module.

This is stronger than a generic “MoE improves capacity” story.
Do not revert to a more implementation-first abstract.

2.2 Three-challenge introduction structure
The explicit challenge paragraphs are valuable and should stay.
They do three jobs at once:
- make the motivation concrete,
- pre-commit the paper to the three method axes,
- and pre-wire the reader to expect the corresponding ablations.

This is exactly the structure that helps later experiment subsections feel justified instead of arbitrary.

2.3 Method section macro-organization
The current 3.x organization is already close to the best final form:
- 3.1 RouteRec Overview
- 3.2 Portable Behavioral Cues
- 3.3 Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing
- 3.4 Behavior-Guided Expert Allocation
- 3.5 Training Objective

This is better than older variants that mixed framework overview, fixed implementation, and objective too early.

3. What to merge in more aggressively from the “method-focused” revision
Even though the uploaded PDFs are the same now, the manuscript should still incorporate the stronger method-writing principles that were developed separately.

3.1 Keep the conceptual method in the main text; move fixed implementation to appendix
Main text should explain:
- what each design axis is,
- why it exists,
- and how it connects to the challenge.

Appendix should explain:
- exact serial layout,
- exact stage order,
- exact H and w values,
- cue bank size,
- router hidden width range,
- temperatures,
- missing-signal handling,
- and kNN consistency details.

Rule of thumb:
- main text = concept + rationale + one representative equation
- appendix = fixed instantiation + reproducibility details

3.2 Do not let the method become too implementation-heavy in 3.3
The serial layout
  self-attention -> macro -> mid -> self-attention -> micro
is useful and should stay,
but it should be presented as “selected paper instantiation” rather than the whole conceptual identity of RouteRec.

Recommended phrasing style:
- first paragraph: explain why stage differences are about temporal evidence and routing granularity;
- second paragraph: explain the selected serial layout as the fixed instantiation used in the paper;
- last sentence: point to Appendix C for stronger scope/order controls.

3.3 Make 3.4 feel like the real third axis
Among the three method parts, 3.4 can still look the most “implementation-ish” if written carelessly.
It should read as the third conceptual axis, not just as a routing formula dump.

What to emphasize in 3.4:
- the group-conditional router is the mechanism that turns cue families into expert allocation,
- it gives a semantic prior over expert organization,
- it preserves comparability across stages,
- and it operationalizes the routing/representation decoupling principle.

What to de-emphasize:
- too much tensor indexing,
- too much discussion of flattening expert indices,
- too much low-level detail that belongs in Appendix A.

4. How to connect Method and Experiments more tightly
This is the most important integration step.
The paper becomes much stronger if every major experiment subsection is visibly “answering” a method subsection.

Recommended crosswalk:

Method 3.2 Portable Behavioral Cues
-> Experiment Q4 lightweight/simple cues
-> Experiment Q3(a) semantic cue design variants
-> Appendix A/Table of representative cue families

Method 3.3 Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing
-> Experiment Q3(b) stage semantics variants
-> Appendix C stage removal / identical-scope / scope swap / layout variants
-> Behavior-slice analysis where gains concentrate in dynamic regimes

Method 3.4 Behavior-Guided Expert Allocation
-> Experiment Q2 routing-control comparison
-> Experiment Q3(c) router-composition variants
-> Appendix D objective variants and Appendix E diagnostics

Method 3.5 Training Objective
-> Appendix D objective + regularization variants
-> Appendix E route consistency / entropy / effective-expert-count diagnostics

This crosswalk should be reflected explicitly in prose.
Example:
- at the end of 3.2: “Q4 and the cue-design ablations later test whether these portable summaries are sufficient and whether their semantic organization matters.”
- at the end of 3.3: “Q3 later evaluates whether the macro/mid/micro decomposition captures meaningful scope separation rather than merely adding more MoE blocks.”
- at the end of 3.4: “Q2 and the router-composition ablations later test whether behavior-guided allocation is stronger and more inspectable than hidden-state-driven alternatives.”

5. Best final organization for the experiment section
The current Q-structured experiment section is a good base. I would keep the Q form.
But the internal logic should be sharpened slightly.

5.1 Q1 should stay narrow
Purpose:
- establish that the full model is competitive enough to justify deeper analysis.

Do not overload Q1 with too much explanation.
It only needs:
- main table,
- short domain-level interpretation,
- and one paragraph explaining that the later subsections are about why the model behaves this way.

5.2 Q2 should be the “decoupling” experiment
Q2 should clearly answer one question only:
- if routing exists, what should drive it?

This is where hidden-only vs mixed vs behavior-guided belongs.
This is the cleanest empirical test of the paper’s core philosophical claim.

5.3 Q3 should be the central structural justification figure
Q3 is the most important non-main-table subsection.
It should explicitly map to the three method axes.

Recommended panel interpretation:
- Q3(a): semantic cue design / family organization matters
- Q3(b): stage semantics / temporal-scope decomposition matters
- Q3(c): group-conditional behavior-guided allocation matters

If there is only one figure that a reviewer remembers after Table 1, it should be this one.

5.4 Q4 should stay portability-oriented, not “another ablation bucket”
Q4 should not compete with Q3.
It should answer a deployment/practicality question:
- do we need rich metadata, or do ordinary logs already suffice?

That means Q4 should focus on:
- remove category cues,
- remove time cues,
- sequence-only or minimal-cue variant,
- retention of full-model gain.

Avoid mixing semantic-shuffle / flat-group / scope-swap controls into Q4. Those belong to Q3 or the appendix.

5.5 Q5 should be the semantic closure of the paper
Q5 should connect back to Figure 1 and the introduction.
It should not just be “more diagnostics”.
Its role is:
- show where gains appear,
- show where routing becomes sharper,
- show that these two co-occur under externally defined behavior slices.

That is the experimental closure of the paper’s story.

6. Best final appendix structure (to avoid overlap)
Right now the main risk is appendix duplication: protocol, method details, structural ablations, diagnostics, and behavior-slice interpretations can easily overlap.
The cleanest appendix structure is:

Appendix A. Method Details and Design Rationale
- fixed paper configuration
- implementation specification
- representative cue family table
- exact stage order / temperatures / missing-field handling
- this appendix supports reproducibility and protects the main text from detail overload

Appendix B. Dataset, Evaluation, Tuning, and Full Results
- processed dataset statistics
- full protocol description
- seen/unseen handling
- sessionization details
- full k in {5,10,20} tables
- tuning/search-space paragraph and possibly compact search-space table

Appendix C. Method-Validation Ablations
- extended cue-structure variants
- stage removal / identical-scope / scope swap
- layout and order controls
- router composition variants

This appendix should answer: “is the chosen architecture really the right one?”

Appendix D. Objective and Regularization Variants
- CE only / CE+cons / CE+z / CE+cons+z / optional balance
- ranking + route consistency + stability indicators
- this avoids confusing routing semantics with auxiliary-loss effects

Appendix E. Diagnostics and Efficiency
- expert-usage heatmaps
- entropy / effective expert count
- route consistency by stage
- feature-bucket route patterns
- if available: compact parameter/runtime cost table

If cost results are weak or noisy, keep only a tiny parameter/runtime anchor and do not overemphasize it.

Appendix F. Behavior-Slice Definitions and Extended Analysis
- explicit slice definitions
- per-dataset slice breakdowns
- gain + concentration plots
- optional qualitative routing cases can live here at the end

Appendix G. Transfer Results (optional)
- keep only if the results are stable and not too distracting
- if transfer is still exploratory or fragile, this appendix can be dropped for the submission version

7. Which appendix sections should be merged or removed
7.1 Fold protocol sensitivity into Appendix B unless it becomes a real table
If “protocol sensitivity” remains conceptual or only lightly populated, do not keep it as a standalone appendix.
Instead:
- add one compact table under Appendix B,
- or one short subsection “Supplementary evaluation views”.

Standalone appendix sections are worth it only when they contain real evidence, not placeholders or promises.

7.2 Fold qualitative routing cases into Appendix F unless they become strong
If the qualitative cases are still lightweight or partially templated, place them at the end of behavior-slice analysis rather than as an independent appendix.

The cleanest interpretation is:
- aggregate slices first,
- then a few concrete cases.

That makes the qualitative examples feel like illustrations of established patterns rather than isolated anecdotes.

7.3 Keep transfer only if it does not weaken focus
Transfer is interesting, but it is not central to the main RouteRec claim.
If space/time/results are uncertain, it should be the first appendix to cut or compress.
The main paper is fundamentally about method validity, not transfer.

9. The tuning paragraph that should be integrated into the paper
This is the most important missing experimental-defense piece.
The current manuscript says “matched early stopping” and “comparable capacity ranges,” but the reader still cannot see how hard each model was tuned.

Recommended solution:
- main text: short but explicit tuning-policy paragraph
- appendix: exact search-space table and selected-config table

Recommended main-text paragraph content:
- each baseline and RouteRec was tuned within a model-specific but comparable discrete search space;
- search spaces were defined around standard backbone-sensitive hyperparameters (embedding/hidden size, dropout, learning rate, weight decay, number of blocks, and model-specific knobs);
- for RouteRec, routing-specific knobs were added on top of the same backbone-scale controls;
- all candidates were selected only by mean validation score over the nine reported metrics;
- the table reports test metrics of the validation-selected run;
- optimization used the same trial budget family and early-stopping rule across models.

Important style point:
Do not claim “fully exhaustive grid search” if the actual execution is Hyperopt/TPE over a discrete set.
Call it:
- “discrete model-specific search space with Bayesian search”
or
- “grid-style discrete candidate space explored with Hyperopt/TPE”.

That wording is honest and strong enough.
