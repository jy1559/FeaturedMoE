\begin{abstract}
Sequential recommendation models have become much better at using temporal order, user history, and side information to predict the next item. More recently, some studies have also introduced Mixture of Experts (MoE) into sequential recommendation and reported promising gains. This opens a natural next question: how can MoE be used more effectively in this setting?

We argue that the key issue is not only whether a model uses experts, but how it decides which experts should handle each input. In sequential recommendation, interaction logs show recurring behavioral patterns such as changes in pace, uncertainty in local transitions, repetition across nearby behavior, and differences in popularity exposure. These patterns suggest useful clues for routing, but they are not explicitly used in most MoE-based sequential recommenders.

\textbf{RouteRec} builds routing signals directly from ordinary interaction logs and uses them to guide expert selection. The model summarizes behavior at multiple temporal scopes, then applies a hierarchical sparse router so that different inputs can activate different expert subsets. Under a shared sessionized temporal protocol across six public datasets, RouteRec achieves the best overall seen-target ranking, with an Avg.~Rank of 1.67 versus 3.56 for the next-best baseline. The clearest gains appear on KuaiRec, LastFM, and Foursquare. Further analysis shows a consistent pattern: RouteRec helps most when the behavioral structure that motivated its routing design is more strongly present in the data. Anonymous code: \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/RouteRec/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Sequential recommendation has advanced through stronger sequence models and richer use of behavioral context for next-item prediction~\cite{wang2021sessionsurvey}. These advances improve what the model represents. They still apply the same shared transformation to every prefix. This leaves an open question in sessionized sequential recommendation: should structurally different behavior always be processed by the same computation path?

Mixture of Experts (MoE) gives a direct way to address that question. It enables \emph{conditional computation}: for each input, the model executes only a data-dependent subset of experts instead of the full shared capacity~\cite{jacobs1991adaptive}. MoE has already been used in recommendation and sequential recommendation~\cite{liu2025fame}. The router determines the resulting \emph{computation path} by selecting which experts are activated.

Recent MoE work often advances in two directions. One direction improves routing mechanics, such as sparse activation efficiency and training stability~\cite{fedus2022switch}. The other direction improves the signal that defines expert allocation, so routing aligns with meaningful variation in the data~\cite{ma2018mmoe}. In our setting, that variation comes from recurring differences in observed behavior that can justify different routes. We refer to the extent to which such variation creates a meaningful need for routed computation as \emph{behavioral routing demand}. Once conditional computation is available, expert allocation is therefore no longer a fixed implementation detail. It becomes a modeling choice. This leads to our central question: in sessionized sequential recommendation, which recurring behavioral axes should define expert allocation?

Sessionized interactions suggest that the answer should come from the sequences themselves. They differ not only in which items appear, but also in how behavior unfolds over time. Some prefixes move at a steady pace, while others speed up or slow down sharply. Some prefixes branch into many plausible next steps, while others stay concentrated. Some show strong repetition or carryover from earlier sessions, while others are shaped more by new exposure. A sessionized view makes these recurring patterns easier to inspect because it places short-term behavior and earlier user history in one frame.

We refer to the extent to which such recurring variation creates a meaningful need for routed computation as \emph{behavioral routing demand}. In this paper, that demand appears repeatedly along four \emph{recurring behavioral axes}: Tempo, which reflects pace and interval dynamics; Focus, which reflects concentration and switching in local transitions; Memory, which reflects repetition and carryover; and Exposure, which reflects popularity level and drift. These axes are motivation-level descriptors, not the router input itself. They tell us where routed computation is likely to matter. A related support condition is \emph{context availability}, which asks whether reusable cross-session evidence is sufficiently available for broader-scope routing.

\textbf{RouteRec} operationalizes these axes as lightweight \emph{behavioral cues} computed directly from ordinary interaction logs. The cues are read on a separate \emph{routing control path}. The \emph{sequential backbone} still handles next-item prediction. Routing is therefore not driven by prediction-optimized hidden states alone. RouteRec computes a cue vector at three routing stages: \emph{macro scope} for broader history, \emph{mid scope} for within-session evolution, and \emph{micro scope} for short local transitions. These cue vectors are organized into four cue families: Tempo, Focus, Memory, and Exposure. A hierarchical sparse router then performs \emph{hierarchical sparse expert allocation}, selecting a small, behavior-dependent expert subset at each stage.

Later experiments show a consistent pattern. RouteRec helps most when local branching is strong and repeated-session context is sufficiently available. The clearest gains appear on KuaiRec, LastFM, and Foursquare. The margin narrows when routing headroom is limited by weaker context availability or by strong shared-path suitability. We use this empirical pattern as supporting evidence for the routing-design view above, not as a post hoc scoring argument.

The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We formulate MoE routing in sessionized sequential recommendation as a routing-design question: expert allocation should be aligned with recurring behavioral axes in interaction logs, not driven by prediction-optimized hidden states alone.
  \item We propose \textbf{RouteRec}, which operationalizes this view through behavioral cue construction, coarse-to-fine multi-stage routing, and hierarchical sparse expert allocation on a routing control path separated from the sequential backbone.
  \item Under a shared sessionized temporal protocol across six public datasets, RouteRec achieves the best overall seen-target Avg.~Rank, with the clearest gains on KuaiRec, LastFM, and Foursquare.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\noindent We organize related work around the modeling question in this paper: what should define expert allocation in sessionized sequential recommendation? We first summarize mainstream sequential recommendation under shared computation. We then review how MoE is introduced in recommendation and sequential recommendation. Finally, we discuss recent MoE advances that clarify the distinction between routing mechanics and routing-signal design.

\subsection{Sequential Recommendation and Shared-Path Baselines}
Sequential recommendation predicts the next item from ordered user interactions \cite{wang2019seqsurvey}. Session-based recommendation is a closely related setting that partitions interactions into short sessions and predicts from current-session context \cite{jannach2022sessions,wang2021sessionsurvey}. Early models established core sequence backbones, including factorized Markov transitions, convolutional architectures, and recurrent architectures \cite{rendle2010fpmc,tang2018caser,hidasi2016gru4rec}. Later models strengthened intent and transition modeling through attention and graph-based designs \cite{li2017narm,liu2018stamp,wu2019srgnn}.

Transformer backbones such as SASRec and BERT4Rec became strong general references \cite{kang2018sasrec,sun2019bert4rec}. Recent variants improve representation quality with contrastive objectives, frequency-aware bias, and state-space sequence modeling \cite{qiu2022duorec,du2023fearec,liu2025sigma}. Across these families, most methods still apply one shared transformation path to all prefixes. This shared-path setup is exactly the context in which our routing question arises.

\subsection{MoE in Recommendation and Sequential Recommendation}
MoE introduces conditional computation through a router that activates only a subset of experts for each input \cite{jacobs1991adaptive,jordan1994hme}. In recommendation, MMoE and PLE show that expert allocation can improve learning when different tasks require different specialization patterns \cite{ma2018mmoe,tang2020ple}. This established the practical value of expert allocation beyond simply enlarging model capacity.

MoE is now also used in sequential recommendation. M3SRec and HM4SR apply experts to multimodal and temporal structure in sequence modeling \cite{bian2023m3srec,zhang2025hm4sr}. FAME uses expert-style factorization to model fine-grained preference facets \cite{liu2025fame}. These results show that expert-based designs can help sequential recommendation. However, routing decisions are still often dominated by hidden representations or modality-specific states. This keeps open the design question we focus on: which recurring behavioral axes in sessionized logs should define expert allocation?

\subsection{Recent MoE Advances for Routing Design}
Recent MoE work often advances along two directions. One direction improves routing mechanics, including sparse activation, dispatch efficiency, and training stability \cite{shazeer2017moe,fedus2022switch,zhou2022expertchoice,zoph2022stmoe}. The other direction improves the signal that defines specialization, so expert allocation follows meaningful structure in data rather than only latent geometry \cite{ma2018mmoe,tang2020ple}.

This distinction motivates RouteRec directly. In sessionized sequential recommendation, recurring differences in pace, transition ambiguity, memory pattern, and exposure pattern create \emph{behavioral routing demand}. RouteRec follows the sparse-routing line for efficient conditional computation, but defines expert allocation through explicit \emph{behavioral cues} aligned with these \emph{recurring behavioral axes}. The cues are computed on a separate \emph{routing control path}, while the \emph{sequential backbone} remains focused on next-item prediction.

\section{Problem and Design Challenges}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Problem Definition}

For each user, let $\mathcal{S}_u=[s_1,\dots,s_M]$ denote the chronologically ordered sequence of sessions, where each session $s_m=[x_{m,1},\dots,x_{m,L_m}]$ contains items interacted with during one continuous activity window. The task is \emph{next-item prediction}: given the current-session prefix $x_{m,1:t}$ and the user's past sessions $[s_1,\dots,s_{m-1}]$, predict the next item $x_{m,t+1}$.

Beyond item identities, interaction logs commonly record when each interaction occurred and what broad category the item belongs to --- temporal timestamps and coarse grouping labels such as category, genre, or artist. Corpus-level popularity statistics can also be derived from the training data. We assume these fields are available in the majority of standard recommendation benchmarks. The method is designed to work best when they are present and to degrade gracefully when some are missing.


\subsection{Design Challenges}
RouteRec starts from a simple premise: recurring behavioral variation in sessionized logs can create \emph{behavioral routing demand}, that is, a meaningful need for routed computation. Three design challenges follow from this premise. They determine whether behavioral routing demand can be translated into stable and behavior-dependent routes, or whether routing collapses back into a near-shared transformation.

\paragraph{\textbf{C1. Cue portability versus informativeness.}}
Behavioral routing demand must be read from signals that are widely available across datasets. In practice, this means item identities, timestamps, and sometimes a coarse grouping field such as category or genre. The difficulty is that such evidence is narrow. A cue built only from these fields may be too weak to differentiate routes, yet a cue that depends on richer metadata is unavailable in many benchmark settings. The first challenge is therefore to construct \emph{behavioral cues} that remain portable while still carrying enough behavioral meaning to support different computation paths.

\paragraph{\textbf{C2. Temporal scope of behavioral evidence.}}
Behavioral routing demand is not temporally uniform. Cross-session repetition and carryover become visible only over multiple past sessions. Within-session concentration builds gradually over the current prefix. Local transition pressure appears only in the most recent interactions before the target. If all of this evidence is merged into one undifferentiated routing signal, slow structural tendency and fast local dynamics become entangled. The second challenge is therefore to align routing decisions with the temporal scope at which each kind of behavioral evidence becomes reliable.

\paragraph{\textbf{C3. Route commitment versus coverage.}}
Even when routing reads meaningful cues at the right temporal scope, it can still fail to produce distinct routes. If weights spread broadly across experts, a repeat-heavy session and a fast exploratory session may activate almost the same mixture. The resulting model behaves little differently from a shared transformation. To avoid this collapse, routing must show \emph{route commitment}: it must concentrate activation on a small, behavior-dependent expert subset. At the same time, it must preserve enough coverage to handle the full range of behavioral regimes seen in practice.

The next section addresses these challenges in the same order. It first identifies what observable evidence the router should read, then explains how that evidence should be separated by temporal scope, and finally shows how it is converted into committed sparse routes.

\section{Proposed Method}
\label{sec:method}

RouteRec is a session-based sequential recommendation model that uses cue-driven sparse routing. It keeps two separate paths: a \emph{sequential backbone} for next-item prediction, and a \emph{routing control path} that reads behavioral cues from interaction sequences and selects experts. This separation keeps routing aligned with behavioral routing demand instead of hidden-state geometry alone.

\subsection{RouteRec Design}
\label{sec:routerec-overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes RouteRec at the framework level. The design follows the three challenges in Section~\ref{sec:problem}: what evidence routing should read, how that evidence should be separated over time, and how it should be turned into distinct routes.

\paragraph{\textbf{C1. Behavioral Cue Construction.}}
For C1, RouteRec fixes the routing evidence to simple metadata that are available before any model-specific transformation. These sources vary substantially across samples in ordinary sequential recommendation logs, and they directly reflect behavior that matters for routing.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Item identities}
  \item \textbf{Timestamps}
  \item \textbf{Optional coarse grouping labels}
  \item \textbf{Training-derived popularity statistics}
\end{itemize}
Section~\ref{sec:feature-construction} turns these metadata into \emph{behavioral cues}.

\paragraph{\textbf{C2. Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing.}}
For C2, RouteRec separates routing evidence by temporal scope, because different behavioral evidence becomes reliable at different ranges.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Recent past sessions}
  \item \textbf{Current-session prefix}
  \item \textbf{Most recent few interactions}
\end{itemize}
Section~\ref{sec:multi-stage-routing} maps these three windows to macro, mid, and micro routing stages.

\paragraph{\textbf{C3. Hierarchical Sparse Expert Allocation.}}
For C3, RouteRec organizes routing so cue information is used selectively rather than through one flat expert pool.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Expert groups aligned with cue families}
  \item \textbf{Within-group expert selection}
  \item \textbf{Sparse top-$k$ activation}
\end{itemize}
Section~\ref{sec:hierarchical-routing} makes this selective use explicit.

Sections~\ref{sec:feature-construction}--\ref{sec:hierarchical-routing} now describe how these three choices are instantiated in the model. Extended layout variants are deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:method-details}.


\subsection{Behavioral Cue Construction}
\label{sec:feature-construction}

The routing control path computes behavioral cues directly from interaction sequences, without passing through the sequential backbone. Given a window of interactions $\mathcal{R}$, a shared summary function $\mathcal{A}(\cdot)$ produces a fixed-size \emph{cue vector} $\mathbf{f} = \mathcal{A}(\mathcal{R})$ from the selected sequence fields.

In the main design, $\mathcal{A}(\cdot)$ summarizes the selected metadata into four \emph{cue families}. The Tempo family captures interaction pace and interval dynamics. The Focus family captures concentration and switching patterns over the coarsest grouping structure available. The Memory family captures repetition, recurrence, and carryover. The Exposure family captures popularity level and drift across the window.

This family structure keeps the routing input compact while preserving the main behavioral distinctions visible in ordinary sequential recommendation logs. Tempo and Memory are supported by item and time signals that are present in nearly all settings. Focus uses the coarsest grouping structure available, and falls back to a fixed missing-signal rule when that field is absent. Exposure uses popularity counts computed from the training corpus. Representative cue definitions are in Table~\ref{tab:appendix-feature-families}; exact dimensions are in Appendix~\ref{app:method-details}.

The same four-family structure is applied at three temporal windows (macro, mid, micro), described in the next subsection. Because the cue template is fixed across all windows and datasets, no dataset-specific feature engineering is required.


\subsection{Coarse-to-Fine Multi-Stage Routing}
\label{sec:multi-stage-routing}

RouteRec separates routing by temporal scope and places one routing stage at each scope. For session $s_m$ at prediction target $t$, the three windows are:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{macro}}_t &= [s_{\max(1,m-H)},\dots,s_{m-1}], \\
\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{mid}}_t   &= [x_{m,1},\dots,x_{m,t-1}], \\
\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{micro}}_t &= [x_{m,\max(1,t-w)},\dots,x_{m,t-1}],
\end{align}
where $H$ is the number of past sessions and $w$ the local window size. Applying $\mathcal{A}(\cdot)$ to each window gives stage-specific cue vectors $\mathbf{f}^{(\mathrm{macro})}_t$, $\mathbf{f}^{(\mathrm{mid})}_t$, $\mathbf{f}^{(\mathrm{micro})}_t$.

Macro reads slower cross-session tendency. Mid tracks how the current session develops. Micro reads the immediate transition context near the target.

The three stages are interleaved with self-attention blocks in the backbone. This keeps routing tied to the temporal role of each stage without expanding the architecture into many competing pathways. In the main configuration:
\[
\underbrace{\text{self-attn}}_{\text{contextualize}} \to \text{macro} \to \text{mid} \to \underbrace{\text{self-attn}}_{\text{refresh}} \to \text{micro}.
\]
Macro and mid are both \emph{sequence-level} decisions. Cross-session tendency and within-session dynamics are properties of the whole session, not of individual positions --- a session that has been repeat-heavy is repeat-heavy everywhere, not just at one token. It therefore makes sense to compute one routing decision per sequence (from averaged cue vectors) and apply it uniformly. Micro, by contrast, is \emph{position-level}: local transition patterns differ at each step, so micro computes a separate routing decision per position. A second self-attention block before micro refreshes token interactions at the point where position-sensitive routing matters most. Full formulations are in Appendix~\ref{app:method-details}.

Section~\ref{sec:hierarchical-routing} describes how expert selection within each stage enforces route commitment.



\subsection{Hierarchical Sparse Expert Allocation}
\label{sec:hierarchical-routing}

RouteRec uses a two-level hierarchical router so cue families and expert specialization remain structurally separated. The router first decides which cue families matter, and then selects experts within the chosen families. This organization also supports \emph{route commitment}: if routing spreads weight across all experts, behaviorally different sessions can still end up with nearly the same mixture.

RouteRec satisfies both requirements through a two-level hierarchical router. The $E$ experts are grouped into $G$ \emph{expert groups} of $C$ experts each ($E = GC$), where each group is aligned with one cue family (Tempo, Focus, Memory, or Exposure). Routing proceeds in two steps: first select which cue families drive computation (family-level), then select specific experts within each chosen family (within-group).

\paragraph{Router formulation.}
The router takes the cue vector $\mathbf{f}^{(s)}_t$ as input and produces two distributions via learned linear projections: a family-level distribution $p^{(s)}_{t,g}$ over expert groups, and a within-group distribution $r^{(s)}_{t,g,c}$ over experts inside each group. Their product $\pi^{(s)}_{t,g,c} = p^{(s)}_{t,g} \cdot r^{(s)}_{t,g,c}$ gives the joint weight for expert $(g,c)$. The factored form matters because it lets the model use cue semantics explicitly: first decide which cue family is most relevant, then select the expert that should respond within that family. The same router structure is shared across all three stages; stages differ only in which cue vector they receive.

\paragraph{Sparse selection.}
Route commitment is enforced by hard top-$k$ selection at both levels: only the top-$k_g$ expert groups and the top-$k_e$ experts within each are active, with their weights renormalized. All other experts contribute nothing to the output. Let $\operatorname{TopK}(\mathbf{v},k)$ be the index set of the $k$ largest entries of $\mathbf{v}$; the sparse group weights $\bar{p}^{(s)}_{t,g}$ and within-group weights $\bar{r}^{(s)}_{t,g,c}$ are:
\begin{equation}
\bar{p}^{(s)}_{t,g} = \frac{p^{(s)}_{t,g}\,\mathbf{1}[g\in \operatorname{TopK}(\mathbf{p}^{(s)}_t,k_g)]}{\sum_{g'} p^{(s)}_{t,g'}\,\mathbf{1}[g'\in \operatorname{TopK}(\mathbf{p}^{(s)}_t,k_g)]},
\label{eq:sparse-route}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\bar{r}^{(s)}_{t,g,c} = \frac{r^{(s)}_{t,g,c}\,\mathbf{1}[c\in \operatorname{TopK}(\mathbf{r}^{(s)}_{t,g,:},k_e)]}{\sum_{c'} r^{(s)}_{t,g,c'}\,\mathbf{1}[c'\in \operatorname{TopK}(\mathbf{r}^{(s)}_{t,g,:},k_e)]}.
\label{eq:sparse-within-group}
\end{equation}
The stage output sums only over active experts ($\hat{\pi}^{(s)}_{t,g,c} = \bar{p}^{(s)}_{t,g}\,\bar{r}^{(s)}_{t,g,c}$):
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{h}}^{(s)}_t = \sum_{g,c}\hat{\pi}^{(s)}_{t,g,c}\,\mathrm{Expert}^{(s)}_{g,c}(\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{in},(s)}_t).
\label{eq:sparse-stage-routing}
\end{equation}
Sparsity here is about commitment, not computational efficiency: it ensures that two sessions with different cues cannot end up on the same route just because the soft weights happen to average out the same way. Dense soft mixing --- where every expert contributes a small nonzero weight --- is evaluated as an ablation in Appendix~\ref{app:sparse-design}. Configuration values ($G$, $C$, $k_g$, $k_e$) are in Appendix~\ref{app:method-details}.


\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{sec:training-objective}

RouteRec is trained end-to-end with one prediction loss and two routing regularizers. The routing regularizers are not optional add-ons: without them, cue-driven routing can degenerate in two distinct ways that the prediction loss alone cannot prevent.

\paragraph{Prediction loss.}
The main objective is standard next-item cross-entropy. Let $\mathbf{z}$ be the final logit vector over all items and $y$ the target item:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}} = -\log \frac{\exp(z_y)}{\sum_{j}\exp(z_j)}.
\]
This loss drives the model to rank the correct next item highly, but it provides no direct signal about whether the routing is cue-sensitive or not.

\paragraph{Route consistency.}
The first failure mode: the router ignores behavioral cues and routes all sessions identically, effectively collapsing back to a shared feed-forward block. Route consistency pushes against this by requiring sessions with similar behavioral cues to produce similar routing distributions. For each stage $s$ and sample $i$, let $\bar{\mathbf{f}}^{(s)}_i = \frac{1}{L_i}\sum_t\mathbf{f}^{(s)}_{i,t}$ be the session-level cue average and $\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}^{(s)}_i = \frac{1}{L_i}\sum_t\boldsymbol{\pi}^{(s)}_{i,t}$ the corresponding average routing distribution. We find each sample's nearest cue neighbor in the mini-batch (pair set $\mathcal{P}_s$) and penalize the divergence between their routing distributions:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}
\sum_{s \in \mathcal{T}}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_s|}
\sum_{(i,j)\in \mathcal{P}_s}
\mathrm{JSD}\!\left(\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}^{(s)}_i,\,\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}^{(s)}_j\right).
\]
This encourages the router to produce routing distributions that reflect the structure of the cue space, not just whatever pattern minimizes the prediction loss.

\paragraph{Routing logit stabilization.}
The second failure mode is subtler: even when the router tries to be cue-sensitive, the pre-softmax logits can grow very large during training. When this happens, the softmax saturates and routing collapses to a near-hard assignment, making small cue differences invisible to the router --- the cue vector stops mattering in practice even though the router nominally reads it. We address this with z-loss \cite{zoph2022stmoe}, which penalizes the scale of the pre-softmax expert logits $a_{i,t,g,c}^{s}$ to keep the routing distribution in a well-behaved range where cue differences can produce meaningfully different outputs:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{z}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}
\sum_{s \in \mathcal{T}}
\frac{1}{N_s}
\sum_{(i,t)\in\mathcal{V}_s}
\!\left(\log \sum_{g,c}\exp a_{i,t,g,c}^{\,s}\right)^{\!2},
\]
where $\mathcal{V}_s$ is the set of valid positions and $N_s=|\mathcal{V}_s|$. The final objective is:
\[
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{cons}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}} + \lambda_{z}\,\mathcal{L}_{z}.
\]
We do not include a load-balancing term. Classical MoE training often penalizes unequal expert usage to prevent collapse onto a small subset. Here the goal is the opposite: behaviorally distinct sessions should activate different, committed routes rather than distributing traffic evenly. Route consistency and logit stabilization together are sufficient for stable, cue-aligned specialization.

\subsection{Main Comparison}
\label{sec:overall-performance}

Table~\ref{tab:main-overall} presents \jm{seen-target(observed in train set at least one time) ranking results (HR@10, NDCG@10, MRR@20) on all six datasets} across six datasets using HR@10, NDCG@10, and MRR@20, with an Avg.~Rank summary across all reported rows. RouteRec achieves the best overall Avg.~Rank (1.67 vs.\ 3.56 for the next-best baseline FDSA), with the clearest margins on Foursquare, LastFM, and the sampled KuaiRec subset. On Beauty and Retail Rocket it remains close to the top, and on ML-1M it is competitive without leading.

\jm{The relative performance of the baselines is largely explained by the nature of our evaluation protocol, not by differences in tuning quality. Because sessions are short and prefixes are recency-sensitive, models that explicitly model temporal structure or recent transitions --- such as SASRec and TiSASRec --- are strong competitors throughout~\cite{kang2018sasrec,li2020tisasrec}. BSARec, which performs well in longer-sequence settings, is less competitive here: its frequency-domain attention mechanism benefits from long, stable interaction histories, which sessionization breaks up~\cite{shin2024bsarec}. DIF-SR, which relies on rich auxiliary side information, gains less because the auxiliary features available across our six datasets are intentionally kept lightweight and consistent, limiting what the side-information branch can add~\cite{xie2022difsr}. Since all methods share the same data, stopping rule, and candidate grid, these differences reflect the protocol and each method's inductive bias, not unequal tuning.}

Seen-target gains are most consistent on Foursquare, LastFM, and KuaiRec (datasets with pronounced behavioral variation across sessions) and narrowest on ML-1M. This pattern is consistent with the core design premise: behavior-guided routing adds the most value when behavioral regimes shift materially across sessions.

\subsection{Routing Control}
\label{sec:routing-control}

A central design question is what the router should use as its input. Two options are available: hidden states, which summarize the sequence but are optimized for next-item prediction rather than behavioral character; and behavioral cues, computed directly from interaction logs on a separate control path. \section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
RouteRec starts from a design question: in sessionized sequential recommendation with accessible cross-session history, can behavioral cues from ordinary interaction logs serve as an explicit control signal for conditional computation, rather than only as inputs for representation enrichment? The experiments support an affirmative reading from three angles. First, RouteRec improves next-item ranking most clearly on behaviorally dynamic datasets and remains competitive elsewhere, although the advantage narrows in more preference-stable settings such as ML-1M. Second, the gain is tied to behavioral cues, staged temporal decomposition, and sparse route commitment rather than indiscriminate expert insertion. Third, compact cues derived from ordinary interaction logs preserve a meaningful share of the benefit, keeping the method practical when side information is limited.

Taken together, the results suggest that behavior-guided routing can be both semantically meaningful and computationally selective. In the main configuration, route-guiding cues, coarse-to-fine routing, and hierarchical sparse expert allocation form a compact interface for conditional computation. Future work can extend that interface through adaptive sparsity budgets, transfer of routing priors across domains, more robust cue construction, and tighter deployment-aware constraints.

More broadly, the paper suggests a useful modeling stance for sessionized recommendation: not every gain from extra capacity should be interpreted as better sequence representation. Some of it can come from exposing a better control interface over when different computations should be used at all. RouteRec's contribution is to make that interface explicit, lightweight, and interpretable.

The main text therefore reports the family structure and representative examples, while this appendix provides the implementation-oriented view.

For reproducibility, the protocol is summarized as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Sort all interactions chronologically within each dataset.
  \item Reconstruct sessions with a dataset-specific inactivity threshold.
  \item Iteratively prune sessions with length below $5$ and items with support below $3$ until the constraints are simultaneously satisfied.
  \item Sort the resulting sessions by session start time.
  \item Assign the earliest 70\% of sessions to train and treat the latest 30\% as a held-out tail.
  \item Build validation and test from the held-out tail under chronology-preserving cleanup.
  \item Report seen-target metrics as the primary view and keep unseen-target behavior as supplementary accounting.
\end{enumerate}

Beauty and Retail Rocket stress sparse e-commerce revisit behavior, Foursquare and KuaiRec emphasize stronger short-horizon intent changes, and LastFM and ML-1M provide longer preference-driven histories. This spread makes it possible to examine whether behavior-guided routing is primarily beneficial in highly dynamic settings or remains competitive when preference structure is more stable.

Signal availability also differs in a way that matters for routing. Beauty exposes item, time, and product-category cues after low-rating removal. Foursquare includes item, timestamp, and venue-category structure with bursty check-in dynamics. KuaiRec offers item, time, and coarse content-side metadata in short-video sessions with strong short-term intent shifts. LastFM provides repeated listening histories with artist-side signals, while ML-1M contributes longer preference-driven sequences with genre information. Retail Rocket is the sparsest side-information setting, relying mainly on anonymous event streams with item and time signals.

We position our setup as a sessionized sequential evaluation protocol assembled from established components, not as the canonical raw-sequence protocol for sequential recommendation~\cite{jannach2022sessions,quadrana2017hrnn,latifi2021sessionaware}. Sessions are reconstructed post hoc with inactivity heuristics, which is standard practice when logs do not provide explicit session boundaries. The thresholds are adapted to the logging regime of each corpus: Foursquare, KuaiRec, LastFM, ML-1M, and Retail Rocket use a 30-minute inactivity threshold, consistent with dense-log session-aware practice~\cite{quadrana2017hrnn,lacic2020jobsessions}, whereas Beauty uses a 14-day threshold because review timestamps are substantially more diffuse. More generally, this threshold choice reflects the observation that sparse and item-poor domains often require longer or more domain-specific notions of a session than dense clickstream data~\cite{borgbruun2022insurance}. After sessionization, we repeatedly remove sessions shorter than $5$ and items with fewer than $3$ interactions until no further pruning is required. This iterative filtering follows the same general rationale as common session-based benchmark preprocessing, where short sessions and very low-support items are filtered to stabilize next-item supervision and evaluation~\cite{li2017narm,wu2019srgnn}.

Session splits are constructed at the session level after sorting by session start time. The earliest 70\% of sessions are assigned to training, and the remaining 30\% are treated as a held-out tail. This tail is first divided into validation and test using stratification over three factors: whether the target item already appears in the prefix, the target item's popularity bucket with respect to the training split, and the session-length bucket. For the final evaluation files, we then merge the held-out sessions, restore chronological order, and re-split them contiguously into equal validation and test halves. This chronology-preserving design is meant to reduce leakage and keep the offline protocol aligned with temporal deployment order~\cite{ji2023leakage,gusak2025timesplit}.

For the primary evaluation view, we report seen-target metrics only. Concretely, in the held-out files, non-target rows whose items are unseen in training are removed, and unseen targets are retained only for supplementary accounting rather than the main comparison. This matches long-standing session-based and session-aware evaluation practice for ID-based recommendation models, which cannot rank targets outside the training vocabulary and therefore typically separate unseen-target behavior from the primary benchmark table~\cite{latifi2021sessionaware}. We report HR, NDCG, and MRR at $k\in\{5,10,20\}$ for all methods.

For the result tables, each method is represented by the run with the highest mean seen-target test score over the nine reported metrics under the same selection rule used throughout the paper.

All methods use the same processed train/validation/test partitions. RouteRec's additional macro, mid, and micro cues are deterministic summaries derived from those same observed histories, so the comparison should be read as evaluating different ways of exploiting the shared sessionized interaction context rather than different access to held-out information.

\subsection{Model Selection and Tuning Protocol}

Because this benchmark is built from a sessionized temporal protocol rather than a canonical raw-sequence leaderboard, the model-selection procedure is stated explicitly. Prior benchmark studies have shown that both baseline quality and model ranking can change materially once methods are re-evaluated under a common framework and retuned under a shared protocol~\cite{latifi2021sessionaware,latifi2022seqstudy}. Accordingly, the comparison does not rely on paper defaults alone.

Our reporting protocol follows three simple rules. First, every model is tuned within a predefined bounded search space that is fixed before the final comparison: learning rate is sampled log-uniformly within a declared dataset-specific interval, while the remaining hyperparameters are drawn from small predeclared candidate sets. Second, these bounded spaces cover a shared core of optimization hyperparameters together with a small number of model-specific controls, so that the search is broad enough to avoid trivial under-tuning but still budgeted tightly enough to remain comparable across methods. Third, the final reported result for every model is the test performance of the configuration that maximizes the mean validation score over the nine reported metrics. When adaptive search is used for computational efficiency, it is restricted to the same predeclared bounds and candidate sets and should therefore be read as an efficient implementation of a controlled bounded search rather than as open-ended retuning~\cite{galuzzi2020bayesopt}.

The final paper track therefore uses bounded search spaces rather than unconstrained retuning. In the paper-facing comparison, baselines and RouteRec are aligned to the same shared optimization pool, and model families differ only through a small number of architecture-specific additions. For RouteRec, the selected three-stage routing layout remains fixed, but depth, hidden dropout, and routing-side controls are still tuned within bounded candidate sets. To keep the appendix concise but still reproducible, Table~\ref{tab:appendix-final-grid} separates the shared optimization pool from the baseline-family and RouteRec-specific additions, and lists the dataset-specific learning-rate intervals explicitly.
