[Priority E] Move defenses from appendix-ish positions into the main text
Three defenses must be more visible:

(1) Handcrafted-feature criticism
Where to put it:

Section 3.2, first paragraph or second paragraph.

Suggested defense direction:
“These cues are not claimed as optimal representation features. Rather, they serve as compact, observable routing proxies. Representation-oriented feature engineering tries to encode what a user prefers; our cues only need to indicate when behavioral regime changes enough to justify a different computation path.”

(2) Capacity-not-routing criticism
Where to put it:

Section 4.5 opening.

Suggested defense direction:
“Sparse RouteRec increases total capacity but restricts executed capacity through selective activation. The practical distinction is therefore active compute per routed decision, not total parameter count alone.”

(3) Protocol criticism
Where to put it:

very early in Section 4.1 / Evaluation Protocol.

Suggested defense direction:
“We intentionally adopt a sessionized temporal protocol rather than a raw-sequence leave-one-out setup because recent work shows that protocol choice can materially alter conclusions, and chronology-breaking or sampled-evaluation choices can produce misleading comparisons.”

This is well supported by prior evaluation work. See refs below.

3. BEAUTY / ML-1M FRAMING: WHAT TO SAY AND WHAT NOT TO SAY

Do NOT use this as the main defense:

“Beauty and ML-1M are explicit-feedback datasets, so timestamps are not real interactions.”
Reason:
That is too brittle.
Beauty still has a strong TiSASRec result, so reviewer can immediately push back.

Safer framing:

The issue is not “explicit feedback bad.”
The issue is “weaker routing demand / sparser proxy for short-horizon behavioral dynamics.”

Target wording:
“RouteRec’s margin narrows on Beauty and ML-1M. We do not read this as contradictory to the method hypothesis. RouteRec is most useful when lightweight observable cues expose meaningful changes in behavioral regime that justify different computation paths. That demand is naturally weaker in settings where observed logs are sparser proxies for immediate interaction dynamics or where within-session behavioral volatility is lower. In such cases, strong shared-path or interval-aware baselines can remain highly competitive.”

This is safer because:

it does not claim timestamps are useless,
it does not require a hard proof that ML-1M is globally “stable,”
it coexists with TiSASRec being strong on Beauty,
it fits the overall story that routing helps most when behavior changes more sharply.

7. CITATION AUGMENTATION PLAN FOR INTRO / RELATED WORK

Principle

Do not dump citations everywhere.
Add citations exactly where they support claims reviewers will challenge.

Best places to strengthen:

(A) Intro claim: “behavior is heterogeneous / user intent changes / short-term needs differ from long-term preference”
Good support:

Jannach, Quadrana, Cremonesi (Session-Based Recommender Systems, Handbook 2022)
Quadrana et al. (HRNN, RecSys 2017)
Wang et al. survey / session survey if already used

Why:

These support the general problem framing that session behavior can reflect varying short-term intent and that sessions differ from long-term preference.

(B) Intro claim: “repeat-heavy vs exploratory behavior / not all prefixes behave the same”
Good support:

RepeatNet (AAAI 2019)

Why:

RepeatNet is a very clean citation for the fact that repeat consumption is common and behavior can differ between repeat and explore modes.

(C) Intro claim: “feature-aware or hybrid models often enrich representation rather than explicitly control computation”
Good support:

Bauer & Jannach 2024 (hybrid session-aware recommendation with feature-based models)
existing FDSA / DIF-SR citations already in the paper

Why:

Bauer & Jannach is useful because it explicitly shows a feature-rich / hybrid session-aware line of work, which is close enough to your framing without overlapping with your routing-control claim.

(D) Related work / intro claim: “recent work models latent or dynamic intent”
Good support if you want a newer citation:

ICSRec / “Intent Contrastive Learning with Cross Subsequences for Sequential Recommendation” (WSDM 2024)
HICL / “Hierarchical Intent Contrastive Learning for Sequential Recommendation” (Expert Systems with Applications 2024)

Why:

These support a modern “dynamic intent / multiple intentions matter” storyline.
Use sparingly: 1 or 2 citations max.
They are helpful if you want the intro to feel more current, but they are not strictly necessary if the intro is already dense.

(E) Protocol / evaluation discussion
Good support:

Latifi et al. 2021
Latifi et al. 2022
Ludewig et al. 2021
plus your existing leakage/splitting refs

Why:

These papers explicitly support the claim that protocol choice, baseline strength, and evaluation setup can strongly affect conclusions in session/sequential recommendation.

Practical recommendation:

Add at most 2–4 citations total beyond what is already there.
Highest-ROI new additions are probably:
RepeatNet
Bauer & Jannach 2024
optionally one of ICSRec or HICL
Do not overstuff the intro.
